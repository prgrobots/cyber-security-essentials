    CIA Triad

    Confidentiality prevents the disclosure of information to unauthorized people, resources, or processes. Integrity refers to the accuracy, consistency, and trustworthiness of data. Availability ensures that information is accessible by authorized users when needed.

    Slide 4¶

    4

    States of data

    The second dimension of the Cybersecurity Cube focuses on the problems of protecting all of the states of data in cyberspace. Data has three possible states: Data in transit Data at rest or in storage Data in process Examples??

    Slide 5¶

    5

    Countermeasures

    Technology : technologies, devices, and products available to protect information systems and fend off cyber criminals. Policies and Practice : build a strong defence by establishing policies, procedures, and guidelines that enable the users to stay safe and follow good practices. Education/training : users must strive to become more knowledgeable about the threats of the cyberspace and establish a culture of learning and awareness.

    Slide 6¶

    6

    Confidentiality

    There are three types of sensitive information: Personal information is personally identifiable information (PII Business information is information that includes anything that poses a risk to the organization if discovered by the public or a competitor. Classified information is information belonging to a government body classified by its level of sensitivity.

    Slide 7¶

    7

    Confidentiality

    Methods used to ensure confidentiality include : data encryption authentication access control

    Slide 8¶

    8

    Confidentiality vs Privacy

    Access to confidential information occurs after confirming proper authorization. Financial institutions, hospitals, medical professionals, law firms, and businesses handle confidential information. Maintaining confidentiality is more of an ethical duty.

    Slide 9¶

    9

    Confidentiality vs Privacy

    Privacy is the appropriate use of data. When organizations collect information provided by customers or employees, they should only use that data for its intended purpose. Most organizations will require the customer or employee to sign a release form giving the organization permission to use the data.

    Access Control - AAA

    Access control refers to the protection schemes that prevent unauthorized access to a computer, network, database, or other data resources. The concepts of AAA involve three security services: Authentication, Authorization and Accounting.

    Slide 13¶

    13

    Authentication

    Users are required to identify themselves: Something you know Something you have Something you are

    Slide 14¶

    14

    Slide 15¶

    Authorization

    Once a user has logged in (ie. been authenticated), authorization services determine which resources users can access and what operations they can perform.

    Slide 16¶

    16

    Accounting

    Accounting keeps track of what users do, including what they access, the amount of time they access resources, and any changes made. Secpol.msc A Windows admin can set up policies to enable system auditing

    Integrity

    Integrity is the accuracy, consistency, and trustworthiness of data during its entire life cycle. Data undergoes a number of operations such as capture, storage, retrieval, update, and transfer. Data must remain unaltered during all of these operations by unauthorized entities.

    Slide 19¶

    19

    Integrity

    Methods used to ensure data integrity include: hashing data validation checks data consistency checks access controls.

    Slide 20¶

    20

    Integrity Checks

    Slide 21¶

    21

    Integrity

    Version control can prevent accidental changes by authorized users. Two users cannot update the same object. Objects can be files, database records, or transactions. For example, the first user to open a document has the permission to change that document; the second person has a read-only version.

    Slide 22¶

    22

    Integrity

    Accurate backups help to maintain data integrity if data becomes corrupted. An organization needs to verify its backup process to ensure the integrity of the backup before data loss occurs.

    Slide 23¶

    23

    Integrity

    Authorization determines who has access to an organization’s resources based on their need to know. File permissions and user access controls ensure that only certain users can modify data. An administrator can set permissions for a file to read-only. As a result, a user accessing that file cannot make any changes.

    Slide 24¶

    24

    Availability

    Availability : the need to maintain availability of information systems and services at all times. Cyberattacks and system failures can prevent prevent legitimate users from accessing and using information systems when needed.

    Slide 25¶

    25

    Availability

    Methods used to ensure availability include : system redundancy system backups increased system resiliency equipment maintenance up-to-date operating systems and software plans in place to recover quickly from unforeseen disasters.

    Slide 26¶

    26

    Five nines

    High availability systems include 3 design principles: Eliminate single points of failure (e.g. hot standbys Provide for reliable crossover (e.g. redundant power supplies and comms) Detect failures as they occur (active monitoring of system and device failures)

    States of Data

    Data in transit Data at rest or in storage Data in process

    Data storage

    A storage device can be local (on a computing device) or centralized (on the network). Examples include: Direct-attached storage (DAS) is storage connected to a computer. (e.g. hard drive or USB flash drive ) RAID NAS SAN Cloud

    Slide 31¶

    31

    Data storage

    Direct-attached storage (DAS) can be one of the most difficult types of storage to manage and control Vulnerable to attacks on local host May include backup data

    ** should not store critical data on DAS

    Slide 32¶

    32

    Data storage

    Network storage systems offer a more secure option. Network storage systems including RAID, SAN and NAS provide greater performance and redundancy. However, network storage systems are more complicated to configure and manage. They also handle more data, posing a greater risk to the organization if the device fails.

    Data in Transit

    Slide 37¶

    Data in Process

    The third state of data is data in process. This refers to data during : initial input modification computation output.

    Slide 38¶

    38

    Data in Process

    Methods to collect data include manual data entry, scanning forms, file uploads, and data collected from sensors. Each of these methods pose potential threats to data integrity. Data corruption during the input process includes data entry errors or disconnected, malfunctioning, or inoperable system sensors, mislabelling and incorrect or mismatched data formats.

    Slide 39¶

    39

    Data in Process

    Data modification refers to any changes to the original data e.g. users manually modifying data, programs processing and changing data, and equipment failing resulting in data modification. Processes like encoding/decoding, compression/decompression and encryption/decryption are all examples of data modification. Malicious code also results in data corruption.

    Slide 40¶

    40

    Data in Process

    Data output refers to outputting data to printers, electronic displays or directly to other devices. The accuracy of output data is critical because output provides information and influences decision-making. Examples of output data corruption include : incorrect use of data delimiters incorrect communication configurations improperly configured printers

    Hardware countermeasures include: Firewall appliances IDS IPS Content filtering

    Slide 44¶

    Technologies

    Network technology countermeasures include: VPN Network access control (NAC) Wireless access point (WAP) security (eg authentication and encryption)

    Slide 45¶

    Technologies

    Cloud based technologies shift the technology from the organisation to the cloud provider. 3 main cloud computing services: SaaS IaaS PaaS Cloud service providers use virtual security appliances that run inside a virtual environment with a pre-packaged, hardened operating system running on virtualized hardware

    Slide 46¶

    46

    People -Training/education

    Formal training program: Make security awareness training a part of the employee’s onboarding process Tie security awareness to job requirements or performance evaluations Conduct in-person training sessions Complete online courses Security awareness should be an ongoing process since new threats and techniques are always on the horizon.

    Slide 47¶

    People - Zero Trust

    47

    Vetting of new employees: Employees have authorized access to the network and sensitive data. To ensure this trust is well placed, through vetting is mandatory. (checking their identity is genuine, police clearances, financial stability and some invasive procedures such as checking their social media for hacktivism or threatening extreme views)

    User Account Behaviour Monitoring: Sounds a bit Orwellian, but a person’s network activity should match their job role/requirements. So, activities are logged and trendlines of the user’s usual behaviour are developed so any anomalies or suspicious activities can be flagged.

    Adequate supervision is exercised: Every employee should be reporting to someone and that someone has the duty to ensure their subordinates are supervised.

    Slide 48¶

    48

    Culture of Cybersecurity

    Cybersecurity awareness culture is an ongoing effort that requires the leadership of upper management and the commitment of all users and employees. Starts with the establishment of policies and procedures by management. E.g. cybersecurity awareness days, banners and signage to increase overall cybersecurity awareness. The creation of cybersecurity orientation workshops and seminars help to increase awareness.

    Slide 49¶

    49

    Policies

    A security policy is a set of security objectives for a company that includes rules of behaviour for users and administrators and specifies system requirements.

    Create a cyber security policy | business.gov.au

    Slide 50¶

    50

    Policies

    A comprehensive security policy accomplishes several tasks: demonstrates an organization’s commitment to security. sets the rules for expected behavior. ensures consistency in system operations, software and hardware acquisition and use, and maintenance. defines the legal consequences of violations. gives security staff the backing of management.

    Slide 51¶

    51

    Standards

    Standards help an IT staff maintain consistency in operating the network. Standards document the technologies that specific users or programs need in addition to any program requirements or criteria that an organization must follow. This helps IT staff improve efficiency and simplicity in design, maintenance, and troubleshooting.

    Slide 52¶

    52

    Guidelines

    Guidelines are a list of suggestions on how to do things more efficiently and securely. They are similar to standards, but are more flexible and are not usually mandatory. Guidelines define how standards are developed and guarantee adherence to general security policies.

    Slide 53¶

    53

    Procedures

    Procedure documents are longer and more detailed than standards and guidelines. Procedure documents usually contain step-by-step instructions and graphics.
